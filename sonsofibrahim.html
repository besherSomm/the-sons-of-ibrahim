Bani Israel

The Children of Israel - Lineage of Prophet Yaqub (Jacob)

Historical Overview

Bani Israel (Children of Israel) refers to the descendants of Prophet Yaqub (AS), also known as Israel. The lineage traces back through Prophet Ishaq (Isaac) to Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham). This section explores the twelve tribes of Israel and their historical significance.

The Twelve Tribes

1. Reuben (Rubil)

Firstborn of Yaqub, mother Leah. Known for his instability.

2. Simeon (Sham'un)

Second son, mother Leah. Known for violence against Shechem.

3. Levi (Lawi)

Third son, mother Leah. Ancestor of the priestly class (Cohen).

4. Judah (Yahuda)

Fourth son, mother Leah. The royal lineage of King David and Jesus (AS).

5. Issachar (Yisakar)

Fifth son, mother Leah. Known as strong asses bearing burdens.

6. Zebulun (Zabulun)

Sixth son, mother Leah. Associated with seafaring and trade.

7. Dan

First son of Bilhah (Rachel's maid). Known for judgment.

8. Naphtali (Naftali)

Second son of Bilhah. Known as a hind let loose.

9. Gad

First son of Zilpah (Leah's maid). Known for warrior tribes.

10. Asher (Asher)

Second son of Zilpah. Known for rich food and delicacies.

11. Joseph (Yusuf)

First son of Rachel, father of Ephraim and Manasseh. The beloved son.

12. Benjamin (Binyamin)

Second son of Rachel, born near Bethlehem. Known as a ravenous wolf.

Prophets Among Bani Israel

Numerous prophets were sent to guide the Children of Israel. Among them: Musa (Moses), Harun (Aaron), Dawud (David), Sulayman (Solomon), Zakariyya (Zechariah), Yahya (John), and Isa (Jesus) – peace be upon them all. Many others, including Ilyas (Elijah), Al-Yasa (Elisha), and Yunus (Jonah), also belonged to this blessed lineage.

Despite receiving continuous divine guidance, Bani Israel frequently rebelled, broke covenants, and even killed prophets. This stands as a grave warning in the Qur'an.

Key Events in Their History

  • Exodus from Egypt: Deliverance from Pharaoh under Prophet Musa; crossing of the Red Sea.
  • The Torah & Golden Calf: Revelation of the Torah at Mount Sinai; the people worshipped a calf in Musa's absence.
  • 40 Years in the Wilderness: Wandering for forty years as punishment; manna and quail were provided.
  • Conquest of Jerusalem: Under Prophet Yusha (Joshua) and later King Dawud & Sulayman; construction of the First Temple.
  • Division & Exile: Split into two kingdoms (Israel & Judah); destruction of Jerusalem; Babylonian captivity.

Divine Favors & Their Response

Allah granted Bani Israel countless blessings: they were chosen over all nations, given clear scriptures, manna and quail, shade of clouds, water from the rock, and a land of milk and honey. Yet time after time they showed ingratitude, arrogance, and transgression.

The Qur'an frequently cites their history as a lesson: "So relate the stories that perhaps they will reflect." (Qur'an 7:176)

The Covenant & The Torah

At Mount Sinai, Allah made a solemn covenant with Bani Israel: uphold the Torah and they would be granted Paradise. However, they broke the covenant repeatedly, distorted scripture, and concealed the truth. Jesus (AS) and Muhammad (SAW) were prophesied in the Torah, yet many rejected them.

The Qur'an addresses them: "O Children of Israel, remember My favor which I bestowed upon you and fulfill My covenant [upon you] that I will fulfill your covenant [of Paradise], and fear [only] Me." (Qur'an 2:40)

Testimony from the Torah

The Torah (Tawrah), the sacred scripture revealed to Prophet Musa (AS), contains extensive accounts of Bani Israel. Below are key passages and classical Jewish interpretations regarding their identity, organization, and covenant.

The Naming of Israel (Genesis 32)

"Then the man said, 'Your name will no longer be Jacob, but Israel (Yisra'el), because you have struggled with God and with humans and have overcome.'"

— Genesis 32:29 (Torah). The patriarch Jacob, after wrestling with an angel, is renamed Israel, meaning 'one who struggles with God and prevails.' His twelve sons thus become the 'Children of Israel' (Bnei Yisrael) [citation:1][citation:4].

The Banners of the Camps (Numbers 2)

The Torah describes the twelve tribes encamping around the Tabernacle (Mishkan) in four divisions, each with a distinct banner. According to the medieval commentator Ibn Ezra (citing Midrash Bamidbar Rabba), these flags bore the images of the Divine Chariot seen by Prophet Ezekiel [citation:6]:

  • 🦁 Judah (East): Lion – "Judah is a lion's cub" (Genesis 49:9).
  • 🐂 Ephraim (West): Ox – "His firstborn bull" (Deuteronomy 33:17), referring to Joseph.
  • 🧑 Reuben (South): Man / Mandrake – recalling the mandrakes (duda'im) found for his mother Leah (Genesis 30:14).
  • 🦅 Dan (North): Eagle – symbolizing swiftness and strength.

These four correspond exactly to the 'living creatures' (Cherubim) of Ezekiel's vision (Ezekiel 1:10). The Midrash states: 'Just as the Holy One, blessed be He, surrounded His Throne with these four beings, so did He command Moses to arrange the camp of Israel' (Bamidbar Rabba 2:10) [citation:6].

From 'Bnei Yisrael' to 'Yisrael' (Deuteronomy)

Rabbi Jonathan Sacks (z"l) observed a profound linguistic shift in the Torah. The phrase 'kol Yisrael' (all Israel) appears eleven times in Deuteronomy, yet it is found nowhere in Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, or Numbers. There, the people are always called 'Bnei Yisrael'—children of Israel [citation:7].

"These are the words Moses spoke to all Israel [le-khol Yisrael]..." — Deuteronomy 1:1

This change signifies that on the plains of Moab, the Israelites were no longer merely a family linked by blood, but a nation bound by covenant and collective responsibility. They were becoming moral adults, transitioning from dependency on miracles to a society governed by Torah [citation:7].

The Firstborns and the Priests

At Mount Sinai, 'the young men of the children of Israel' offered sacrifices (Exodus 24:5). Classical commentators (Rashi, Ibn Ezra, Ramban) identify these youths primarily as the firstborns, who originally held the priestly role. However, after the sin of the Golden Calf, this service was transferred. The Torah states [citation:9]:

"Behold, I have taken the Levites from among the children of Israel instead of every firstborn that opens the womb among the children of Israel; therefore the Levites shall be Mine." — Numbers 3:12 (Torah)

This substitution is directly linked to the Exodus: 'For all the firstborn are Mine; on the day that I struck down all the firstborn in Egypt, I sanctified them for Myself' (Numbers 3:13). Thus, the Torah itself establishes that the deliverance from Pharaoh consecrated Bani Israel exclusively to God [citation:9].

These Torah passages—from Jacob's renaming, to the banners in the wilderness, to the covenant at Sinai and the substitution of the Levites—form the scriptural foundation upon which later Jewish, Christian, and Islamic understandings of Bani Israel are built. The Qur'an affirms these narratives as part of the 'Book of Moses' (Qur'an 11:17, 46:12), inviting believers to reflect on the continuity of divine guidance.

Arab Tribes

The Descendants of Ismail (Ishmael) - Genealogy, Genetics, and Linguistic Heritage

Ismaili Lineage

The Arab tribes primarily descend from Prophet Ismail (AS), son of Prophet Ibrahim (AS) and Hajar (Hagar). Genealogists trace the 'Arabized Arabs' (al-‘Arab al-musta‘ribah) to Ismail through his son Qaydhar (Kedar). The central genealogical junction is Adnan, the 21st great-grandfather of Prophet Muhammad (PBUH), from whom all northern (Adnanite) tribes originate. Southern (Qahtanite) Arabs descend from Qahtan (biblical Joktan), son of Eber, and represent the 'Pure Arabs' (al-‘Arab al-‘aribah).

Adnanite Tribes (Northern Arabs)

Quraysh (Fihr ibn Malik)

Lineage: Adnan → Ma'ad → Nizar → Mudar → Ilyas → Mudrikah → Khuzaymah → Kinanah → Al-Nadr → Malik → Fihr (Quraysh).

Major Clans:

  • Banu Hashim: Clan of Prophet Muhammad (PBUH), Ali, and Abbas. Includes the Hashimids and Abbasid caliphs.
  • Banu Umayya: Umayyad caliphs; descendants of Umayya ibn Abd Shams.
  • Banu Makhzum: Elite military clan; included Khalid ibn al-Walid.
  • Banu Zuhrah: Clan of Aminah bint Wahb (mother of the Prophet).
  • Banu Taym: Clan of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq.
  • Banu Adi: Clan of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

Y-DNA: J1-P58 (60-84%) – the Semitic marker associated with the expansion of Arabian tribes.

Banu Tamim

Descended from Tamim ibn Murr ibn 'Udd ibn Tabikhah. One of the largest Adnanite confederations, known for eloquence and poetry.

Subdivisions:

  • Banu Darim: Noble clan; included the pre-Islamic poet Al-Nabighah al-Dhubyani.
  • Banu Yarbu': Clan of the famous poet Al-Farazdaq.
  • Banu Sa'd: Harbi branch; foster-family of the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH).
  • Banu Hanzalah: Included the judge and companion Abu Uthman al-Nahdi.

Rabi'a Confederation

Descended from Rabi'a ibn Nizar. Includes Banu Hanifa (Yamama), Banu Bakr, and Banu Taghlib. The tribe of Musaylimah the Liar; also the tribe of the pre-Islamic poet 'Amr ibn Kulthum.

Qahtanite Tribes (Southern Arabs)

According to classical genealogists, Qahtan (biblical Joktan) is the progenitor of all 'Pure Arabs'. The Qahtanite confederation split into two main branches: Himyar and Kahlan.

Himyar

Includes Quda'a, Thu Raidan, Malik, and Ka'b. The great Himyarite kingdoms of pre-Islamic Yemen.

Y-DNA: J1-P58 (subclade L858) – 73-85% in Yemen.

Kahlan

Includes: Azd (Shanua), Madh'hij, Hamdan, Khu'lan, Tayyi', Lakhm, Judham, Kinda, Amilah, Ash'ar, and Bajilah.

The Azd migrated to Oman, Syria, and Iraq; from them came the Ghassanids (Syrian vassals of Byzantium) and the Ansar of Medina (Aws and Khazraj).

Lesser-Known & Contemporary Tribes

Shihuh (Al Shehhi)

Mountain tribe of Ras Al Khaimah and Musandam. Divided into Bani Shatair and Bani Hadiyah. Their dialect (Shehhi) is unique, containing substratum elements from ancient non-Arabic languages (possibly Persian or South Arabian). Bertram Thomas (1928) described it as a 'strange tongue'.

Y-DNA: J1-P58 + T1-CTS6507 (preliminary).

Habus

Originally a section of Bani Hadiyah (Shihuh). Speak the Shehhi dialect.

Za'ab

Coastal tribe of Ras Al Khaimah; traditional inhabitants of Jazirat al-Hamra. Pearl divers and seafarers.

Al Ali

Prominent coastal tribe of the UAE.

Bani Khalid

Historically ruled eastern Arabia. Genetically associated with haplogroup T1-CTS6507.

Al Balawi (Quda'a)

One of the oldest tribes; associated with basal haplogroup A-ALK776 (ancient lineage).

Dialects & Linguistic Heritage

The tribal dialects of Arabia form the foundation of the Arabic language. Versteegh (1997) and Ingham (1982) classify modern Bedouin dialects into four primary groups, each preserving archaic features lost in sedentary varieties:

North-East Arabian

Tribes: ʿAniza, Šammar. Features: 'gaháwa' syndrome (resyllabification), affrication of /k/ and /q/, retention of tanwin as indefinite marker. Most conservative Najdi type.

South-West Arabian

Yemen, Hadramaut, Aden. Features: k-perfect (katabku), preservation of South Arabian substratum, Himyaritic relics.

Ḥijāzī (West Arabian)

Bedouin dialects of Hijaz & Tihama; urban Meccan dialect. No affrication; /q/ = /g/ in Bedouin, /q/ in urban? Features shared with Upper Egypt.

North-West Arabian

Negev, Sinai, southern Jordan. Distinct group per Palva (1991). Retain interdentals, gender distinction in plural.

Shehhi (Linguistic Isolate)

Dialect of the Shihuh tribe (Ras Al Khaimah/Musandam). Bertram Thomas (1928) noted it was 'almost exclusively a compound of corrupt Persian and Arabic.' Members are bilingual in a 'sing-song Arabic' and their own distinct speech. Even today, other Arab tribes find Shehhi difficult to understand.

Genetic Ancestry (Y-DNA Haplogroups)

Modern population genetics has confirmed and refined classical genealogical divisions. Major haplogroups among Arab tribes:

J1-P58 (J1e)

Dominant in Adnanites (Quraysh, ʿAniza, Šammar): 60-84%. Also high in Qahtanites (Yemen): 73-85% (subclade L858). The 'Semitic marker' linked to the expansion of pastoralists from Arabia.

T1-CTS6507

Associated with Bani Khalid (>30% in project members). Also found in Shihuh and some Yemeni tribes. Represents an ancient pre-J1 lineage.

A-ALK776

Basal haplogroup found in Al Balawi (Quda'a). One of the oldest Y-lineages in Arabia, predating the J1 expansion.

E1b1b1 (E-M34)

Found in Egyptian and Palestinian Arab populations (18-46%), often alongside J1. Represents a North African substratum.

Classical Genealogical Works

Qalqashandi

Nihayat al-arab fi ma'rifat ansab al-'Arab – A comprehensive dictionary of Arab genealogy.

Ibn Hazm

Jamharat ansab al-'Arab – One of the most important classical works on Arab lineages.

Al-Suwaydi

Saba'ik al-dhahab fi ma'rifat qaba'il al-'Arab – A monumental lithographed genealogical tree extending from Biblical times to the Ottoman era (1864). One of the rarest and most important works on Arab tribes.

DNA & Genetics

Scientific Perspectives on Abrahamic Lineages - Y-Chromosome Haplogroups, Migration Timelines, and Population Genetics

Genetic Genealogy

Modern genetic research offers fascinating insights into the historical populations of the Middle East. While DNA cannot prove specific prophetic lineages, it can reveal migration patterns and connections between populations historically associated with Abrahamic traditions. The Y-chromosome, passed from father to son, is particularly useful for tracing patrilineal ancestry and testing genealogical claims against scientific data.

Major Haplogroups of the Abrahamic Lands

J1 (J-M267) - The Arabian-Semitic Marker

Origin: Western Asia, approximately 10,000-36,000 years before present. The oldest diversity appears in the Taurus-Zagros mountains (northern Fertile Crescent) [citation:3][citation:9].

Distribution: Yemen (80%), Saudi Arabia (64%), Qatar (58%), Negev Bedouins (62%), Palestinian Arabs (38.4%), Syria (30%), Lebanon (25%), Algeria (35%), Tunisia (31%), Egypt (20%). Collapses sharply at non-Arabic borders: Turkey (9%), Iran (9.5%) [citation:2][citation:3][citation:9].

TMRCA (Time to Most Recent Common Ancestor): Jordan: 28ka (16-54ka), Lebanon: 31ka (18-58ka), Palestinians: 31ka (18-58ka), Syria: 35ka (21-64ka), Egypt: 29ka (15-60ka), Morocco: 33ka (17-69ka) [citation:1].

J2 (J-M172) - The Mesopotamian & Mediterranean Marker

Origin: Northern Fertile Crescent, Anatolia, and the Levant. Associated with the spread of agriculture (8,500 ± 3,500 years ago) [citation:4].

Distribution: Turkey (25%), Iraq (25%), Lebanon (25%), Kurdistan (28%), Greece, Italy, Iberia, Caucasus, Iran, Central Asia, South Asia. J2a is linked to ancient Crete and Minoan civilization [citation:4].

TMRCA: Armenia: 34ka (19-69ka), Iran: 35ka (19-68ka), Jordan: 28ka (16-54ka), Lebanon: 33ka (20-62ka), Palestinians: 32ka (19-60ka), Turkey: 34ka (19-65ka), Tunisia: 32ka (16-72ka) [citation:1].

E1b1b (E-M215) - The African-Mediterranean Marker

Origin: Northeast Africa or the Middle East, approximately 50,000-55,000 years before present. The most characteristic Y-haplogroup of Africa, with significant presence in the Mediterranean [citation:5].

Distribution: Egypt, Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia, Libya, Horn of Africa, Balkans, Iberia. Among Egyptian and Palestinian Arab populations: 18-46% [citation:2][citation:5].

TMRCA: Armenia: 40ka (18-92ka), Iran: 36ka (19-73ka), Jordan: 32ka (18-62ka), Lebanon: 32ka (17-64ka), Palestinians: 31ka (18-57ka), Syria: 29ka (16-60ka), Turkey: 32ka (18-65ka) [citation:1].

T (T-M184) - The Ancient Pre-Semitic Lineage

Origin: Branched from haplogroup K. Most lineages traveled east into Asia and the Pacific, but some journeyed into the Middle East and Europe [citation:8].

Distribution: Bani Khalid tribe (eastern Arabia), Shihuh (Musandam), Yemen, and scattered populations across the Middle East [citation:8][citation:10].

Significance: Represents an ancient pre-J1 lineage, preserved in isolated populations and certain tribal groups.

R1a - The Eurasian Steppe Marker

Distribution in Arabia: Present in the Arabian Peninsula at low frequencies (3,750 years to common ancestor). Found among certain Bedouin and settled populations, reflecting ancient gene flow from Central Asia [citation:6].

G (G-M201) - The Caucasus-Anatolian Marker

Origin: The first branch of F, G traveled from the Middle East into both Europe and South Asia. European lineages spread along the Mediterranean [citation:8].

Significance: Found among Anatolian, Caucasian, and Levantine populations. Present in some Jewish and Arab groups at low frequencies.

J1 Subclades: The Semitic Expansion Decoded

The most detailed phylogenetic tree of J1 reveals multiple layers of migration, from the Neolithic pastoralists to the historic Arab expansion. The P58 marker (J1c3) dominates J1 in Semitic-speaking populations [citation:9][citation:10].

J1* (Pre-P58) - The Paleolithic Northern Layer

Highest frequencies in the Caucasus and eastern Turkey. Found among Cushitic Oromo in Ethiopia (all J1 chromosomes are J1*). Represents the oldest J1 stratum, pre-dating the Semitic expansion [citation:3][citation:9].

J1c3 (P58) - The Proto-Semitic Marker

Defining mutation: P58/Page08. Originated in the northern Fertile Crescent (NE Syria, N Iraq, E Turkey) ~9,000-10,000 BP. Spread southward with pastoral nomads following rainfall patterns. Dominant form of J1 among Semitic speakers [citation:3][citation:9][citation:10].

J1c3d (L147.1) - Diagnostic Marker of Semitic Expansion from Arabia

Accounts for +90% of J1-P58. Found in high frequencies in Yemen, among Cohens (Jewish priestly lineage), and Ethiopia. Represents the major Semitic expansion from the Arabian Peninsula [citation:10].

J1c3d2 (L222.2) - The Adnani Marker

Accounts for the majority of L147.1 in Saudi Arabia, Sudan, and North Africa. Seems to be an exclusively Adnani (northern Arab) marker. Associated with the historic Arab expansion and Islamization [citation:10].

J-M368 - The Relict Northern Branch

Subclade of J-P58 with DYS388=13 and YCAII=19-22. Most common in Caucasus and Anatolia. A relatively old offshoot that did not take part in the recent waves of J1 expansion [citation:3][citation:9].

J2 Subclades: Farmers, Seafarers, and Empires

J2a (M410) - Mesopotamian Expansion

Paleolithic expansion out of Mesopotamia. Subclade L581 remained in Mesopotamia through the Paleolithic and expanded during the Neolithic agricultural revolution [citation:10].

J2a1b (M67) - Western Expansion

Expanded west of the Euphrates valley. Moderate distribution in Anatolia, Caucasus, East Mediterranean, and the ancient Greek world. M92 is the most important subclade, linking Anatolia and southern Italy [citation:4][citation:10].

J2b (M12) - Balkan-Anatolian Corridor

Upstream lineages found in Anatolia, Arabia, Europe, and India. Subclade M241 accounts for the majority. L283 emerged in the Balkans in the Neolithic and has a pan-European low frequency distribution [citation:4][citation:10].

J2a1h (M319) & The Cohen Modal Haplotype

J2-L254 is an exclusive marker of the J2 Cohens. TMRCA for Cohen Modal Haplotype J2: 1,400 years before present (Klyosov 2008). J2a1h (M319) is an Eastern Anatolian population that expanded to Europe in the Neolithic and expanded in the ancient Greek world [citation:6][citation:10].

E1b1b Subclades: Three Strata of Mediterranean History

E1b1b1a1b (V13) - Balkan Neolithic

Associated with the Neolithic expansion of agriculture from the Balkans. Highest frequencies in the Balkans and Southern Europe. Represents the first major E1b1b migration into Europe [citation:5].

E1b1b1b1a (M81) - Berber Marker

Probably originated in the Horn of Africa and migrated to the Maghreb. Average frequency of 45% among Berbers. Very common in Iberia: Extremadura (15.5%), southern Portugal (11%), NW Castile (10%). Highest frequency in Europe is among Pasiegos (30%), an isolated community in Cantabria [citation:5].

E1b1b1b2a (M123) - Phoenician & Ethiopian Marker

Most frequent in Ethiopia, where it appears to have originated. Later accompanied Phoenician maritime trade expansion throughout the Mediterranean [citation:5].

The Cohen Modal Haplotype (CMH)

The Cohen Modal Haplotype is a specific Y-chromosome signature associated with the Jewish priestly lineage (Kohanim). It appears in two distinct haplogroups:

J1 CMH

Two lineages: 4,000 years BP and 1,050 years BP (Klyosov 2008). Belongs to J1c3d (L147.1). Found in Yemenite Jews (30%), Ashkenazi (20%), Sephardi (12%) [citation:6][citation:9].

J2 CMH

TMRCA: 1,400 years BP (Klyosov 2008). Defined by marker J2-L254. Exclusive marker of the J2 Cohen lineage [citation:6][citation:10].

These findings demonstrate that the Cohen priesthood is not a single lineage but a cluster of related lineages within both J1 and J2, reflecting the deep genetic diversity of ancient Semitic populations.

Migration Routes and Gene Flow

Arabian Corridor

A 2025 study using PowerPlex Y23 on 379 Qatari males revealed the main migration route was from Yemen to Kuwait through Qatar. The Arabian Peninsula served as a crossroads for population movements since the Neolithic [citation:7].

Levantine-Mediterranean Littoral

J2 and E1b1b show high concentrations along Mediterranean coasts, suggesting maritime dispersal associated with Phoenician, Greek, and Neolithic seafaring expansions [citation:4][citation:5].

Zagros-Taurus Pastoral Circuit

J1c3 (P58) spread with pastoral nomads who migrated seasonally between the Zagros-Taurus mountains and the Levant, following rainfall patterns. This Neolithic mobility laid the foundation for Semitic linguistic expansion [citation:3][citation:9].

Research Ethics

We emphasize that genetic research should be approached with humility and respect for religious traditions. Genetic data provides population-level insights but cannot confirm or invalidate individual religious beliefs or specific ancestral claims to prophetic lineages. The scientific study of Y-chromosome haplogroups reveals deep histories of migration and kinship, but faith and genealogy operate on different planes of truth.

Primary Scientific Studies (Peer-Reviewed)

The following studies provide the direct scientific evidence for the migration timelines, haplogroup origins, and TMRCA estimates cited throughout this page. Each link is a permanent, citable record (DOI or PubMed).

The emergence of Y-chromosome haplogroup J1e among Arabic-speaking populations

Authors: Chiaroni J, King RJ, Myres NM, et al.

Journal: European Journal of Human Genetics. 2010 Mar;18(3):348-53.

Link: PubMed: 19826455 | DOI: 10.1038/ejhg.2009.166

🔬 Key finding: Expansion time of J1e estimated at ~10,000 years BP. Highest diversity in Zagros/Taurus mountains. Parallels the spread of rain-fed agriculture and Semitic-speaking pastoralists [citation:3][citation:7].

Qatar and the Middle East: Insights from a Y-STR Study Using PowerPlex Y23

Authors: Almohammed EK, Hadi A, Mghirbi T, Marzook K, Elramahi I, Lazim H.

Publication: 30th Congress of the International Society for Forensic Genetics (2025). Universidade de Santiago de Compostela, pp. 265-273.

Link: Edge Hill University Research Archive

🔬 Key finding: Analysis of 379 Qatari males + 11,305 individuals from 134 populations. Bayesian coalescence modeling reveals the main migration route was from Yemen to Kuwait through Qatar [citation:5].

Origin, diffusion, and differentiation of Y-chromosome haplogroups E and J

Authors: Semino O, Magri C, Benuzzi G, et al.

Journal: American Journal of Human Genetics. 2004 May;74(5):1023-34.

Link: Semantic Scholar (Full Text) | DOI: 10.1086/386295

🔬 Key finding: Investigated >2400 subjects from 29 populations. Revealed spatial patterns consistent with a Levantine/Anatolian dispersal route to southeastern Europe. Foundational study for E1b1b and J2 phylogeography in the Mediterranean [citation:2].

Whole Y-chromosome sequences reveal an extremely recent origin of E-M183 (M81)

Authors: Solé-Morata N, García-Fernández C, Urasin V, Bekada A, Fadhlaoui-Zid K, Zalloua P, Comas D, Calafell F.

Journal: Scientific Reports. 2017 Nov 20;7(1):15941.

Link: Nature: s41598-017-16271-y | PubMed: 29158553

🔬 Key finding: Used whole Y-chromosome sequencing of 32 North Africans. TMRCA for E-M183: 2,000-3,000 years ago. Evidence points to a local origin in NW Africa rather than the Near East, coinciding with the Roman period or Arab expansion [citation:1].

DNA Genealogy, Mutation Rates, and Historical Evidences in Y-Chromosome

Author: Klyosov AA.

Journal: Nature Precedings. 2008 Dec 29.

Link: Nature: npre.2008.2733.1

🔬 Key finding: Direct source for TMRCA of Cohen Modal Haplotype (CMH). J1: 4,000 and 1,050 years BP (two lineages). J2: 1,400 years BP. Also provides dates for Arabian Peninsula R1a1 (3,750 BP) [citation:4].

Note: TMRCA (Time to Most Recent Common Ancestor) estimates vary between studies due to different mutation rate calibration methods (evolutionary vs. genealogical). The dates cited here are those used in the main page content.

Android Implementation

Example code for checking updates in your Android app: 

public void checkForUpdates() {
    String url = "https://sonsofibrahim.app/api/latest_version?id=com.sonsibrahim.app ";
    
    JsonObjectRequest request = new JsonObjectRequest(
        Request.Method.GET, url, null,
        response -> {
            int versionCode = response.getInt("versionCode");
            if (versionCode > BuildConfig.VERSION_CODE) {
                showUpdateDialog(response);
            }
        },
        error -> Log.e("Update", "Error checking for updates")
    );
    
    Volley.newRequestQueue(context).add(request);
}

Update Flow

  1. App calls API endpoint on startup
  2. Compares versionCode from API with local version
  3. If newer version exists, show update dialog
  4. User selects 'Update Now' or 'Later'
  5. If updating, download APK from downloadUrl
  6. Install APK using FileProvider (Android 7.0+)

About Us

Our mission to preserve and share Abrahamic history

Our Mission

Sons of Ibrahim is a non-profit educational initiative dedicated to documenting and sharing the rich genealogical history of the Abrahamic faiths. We believe that understanding our shared heritage through the lineage of Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) can foster greater understanding and respect between communities.

Sources & Methodology

Our content is compiled from authentic Islamic sources (Qur'an, Hadith, classical tafsir), Biblical genealogies, established historical records, and peer-reviewed genetic studies. We maintain academic rigor while presenting information accessible to general readers.

Our History

Sons of Ibrahim was founded with the vision of creating a comprehensive, accessible digital resource for understanding the genealogical connections between the Abrahamic faiths. Recognizing the fragmented nature of historical records and genealogical information scattered across various religious and academic sources, our founders came together to build a unified platform that respects all three major Abrahamic traditions: Islam, Christianity, and Judaism.

Since our inception, we have grown into a global initiative with contributors from multiple faith backgrounds, academic disciplines, and geographic regions. Our platform now serves thousands of users seeking to explore their heritage and understand the shared history of Abrahamic peoples.

Our Goals

1. Comprehensive Genealogical Database

Build and maintain an exhaustive digital archive of the lineages connecting Ibrahim (Abraham) through his descendants across all three Abrahamic faiths, verified against multiple authoritative sources.

2. Educational Accessibility

Make complex genealogical and historical information accessible to users of all backgrounds and educational levels through intuitive interfaces, multiple languages, and multimedia content.

3. Interfaith Understanding

Foster mutual respect and understanding between communities by highlighting shared genealogical heritage and demonstrating the deep connections between Islamic, Christian, and Jewish traditions.

4. Scientific Validation

Integrate modern genetic research with traditional genealogical records to provide evidence-based insights into human migration patterns and population history related to Abrahamic peoples.

5. Community Engagement

Create interactive tools that allow users to explore their own family histories, contribute verified genealogical information, and participate in a global community dedicated to preserving this shared heritage.

Our Vision

We envision a world where the genealogical connections between Abrahamic peoples are not only well-documented and scientifically validated, but also widely understood and celebrated as a foundation for peace, cooperation, and mutual respect. Through Sons of Ibrahim, we aim to demonstrate that despite theological differences, the followers of Islam, Christianity, and Judaism share a profound common ancestry that transcends borders, cultures, and time.

Our Values & Principles

Accuracy

We commit to rigorous fact-checking and verification against authoritative sources from all three faiths.

Inclusivity

We respect and value perspectives from Islamic, Christian, Jewish, and secular academic viewpoints.

Transparency

All sources, methodologies, and decisions are openly documented and available for community review.

Non-Profit Mission

We operate solely to educate and preserve heritage, with no commercial agenda or bias toward any faith.

Family Tree of Ibrahim

The Complete Genealogy of Prophet Ibrahim (AS) - From His Three Wives to the Great Nations

Overview of Ibrahim's Family

Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) is the patriarch of three great nations. Through his three wives, he fathered sons who became the ancestors of the Arabs, the Israelites, and several other nations. According to Islamic tradition and biblical genealogy, Ibrahim had eight sons: two from Hagar (Ismail and his descendants), one from Sarah (Ishaq), and six from Keturah. The Qur'an mentions Ibrahim as 'Khalilullah' (Friend of Allah) and describes his family as the foundation of monotheistic faith.

The Three Wives of Ibrahim

Sarah (Sarai)

Ibrahim's first wife and half-sister (daughter of his father Terah by another wife). She bore Isaac (Ishaq) at the age of 90, fulfilling God's promise. The matriarch of the Israelite nation through whom all prophets of Bani Israel descended.

Hagar (Hajar)

Sarah's Egyptian handmaid who became Ibrahim's second wife. She bore Ismail (Ishmael), the father of the Arab nation. The Qur'an describes her search for water in the desert, leading to the discovery of Zamzam well in Mecca.

Keturah (Qatoora)

Ibrahim's third wife, whom he married after Sarah's death. She bore six sons who became ancestors of various nations in the east, including the Midianites. Some traditions identify her with Hagar, while others consider her a distinct wife.

Lineage from Sarah: Bani Israel (The Israelites)

Ishaq (Isaac)

The promised son, born when Ibrahim was 100 and Sarah was 90. The second prophet in this lineage. Married Rebekah (Rifqah) and had twin sons.

Ya'qub (Jacob) - Israel

The younger twin who received the birthright and blessing. Father of the Twelve Tribes of Israel. Also known as Israel, meaning 'one who struggles with God.'

Prophets from this Lineage

All Israelite prophets descended from this line: Yusuf (Joseph), Musa (Moses), Harun (Aaron), Dawud (David), Sulayman (Solomon), Zakariyya, Yahya (John), and Isa (Jesus) - peace be upon them all.

Lineage from Hagar: Ismail (Ishmael) - Father of the Arabs

Ismail (Ishmael)

Ibrahim's firstborn son, born when Ibrahim was 86. The ancestor of the Arab nation and the Ishmaelites. The Qur'an mentions him as a prophet and describes how he helped his father build the Kaaba in Mecca.

The Twelve Princes of Ismail

As promised by God to Ibrahim, Ismail became the father of twelve princes (tribal leaders). Their descendants formed great nations across the Arabian Peninsula.

The Twelve Sons of Ismail
• Nebaioth (Nabit) - Father of Nabataeans
• Kedar (Qaydhar) - Ancestor of Quraysh
• Adbeel

Quraysh and Prophet Muhammad (PBUH)

From Kedar (Qaydhar) descended the Quraysh tribe, the noble tribe of Prophet Muhammad (PBUH). The lineage continues: Kedar → Nabit → Salma → Fihr (Quraysh) → Malik → Kilab → Qusayy → Abd Manaf → Hashim → Abdul-Muttalib → Abdullah → Muhammad (PBUH).

Other Arab Tribes from Ismail

The descendants of Nebaioth became the Nabataeans, who established a powerful kingdom in Petra (Jordan). Other sons' descendants intermarried with local populations and formed various Arab tribes across Arabia.

Lineage from Keturah: The Eastern Nations

The Six Sons of Keturah

After Sarah's death, Ibrahim married Keturah who bore him six sons. Ibrahim gave them gifts and sent them eastward away from Isaac, toward the east country (Arabia/Jordan region).

Zimran

Ancestor of some Arabian tribes

Jokshan (Yaqshan)

Father of Sheba and Dedan (trading peoples)

Medan

Associated with northern Arabian tribes

Midian (Madyan) ⭐

The Midianites - famous traders, Moses lived with them

Ishbak

Ancestor of northern Arabian groups

Shuah (Shu'ah)

Associated with Syrian/Northern Arabian region

The Midianites

The most prominent descendants of Keturah. Prophet Musa (Moses) lived with the Midianites after fleeing Egypt, married Jethro's daughter, and received prophecy there. The Midianites were famous traders of gold, incense, and textiles.

Sheba and Dedan

Grandsons of Keturah through Jokshan. The people of Sheba became famous for frankincense and myrrh trade. The Queen of Sheba who visited Prophet Sulayman (Solomon) likely came from this lineage.

The Abrahamic Covenant

God's Promise to Ibrahim

God promised Ibrahim that he would be 'a father of many nations' (Genesis 17:5). This was fulfilled through all three lineages: through Isaac came the Israelites and their prophets; through Ishmael came the Arab nation and Prophet Muhammad (PBUH); through Keturah's sons came various nations including the Midianites.

The Two Great Nations

The Qur'an (14:35-37) mentions Ibrahim's prayer: 'Our Lord! I have settled some of my offspring in a valley where there is no cultivation, by Your Sacred House... so that they may establish prayer and make hearts turn toward them.' This prayer was answered through both Isaac's line (Bani Israel and their prophets) and Ishmael's line (the Arab nation and the final prophet Muhammad PBUH).
